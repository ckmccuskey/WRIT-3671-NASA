Through Time and Space

The Visual Rhetoric of NASA's Apollo Program Emblems

Colleen McCuskey

Introduction and Project Overview

In culmination of a semester’s worth of study in visual rhetoric and design, this project will provide an in-depth, grounded scholarly critique and analysis of how the visual choices of the NASA emblems were, and are, effective for their intended audiences.

Thesis

The mission patches of 3 NASA Apollo launches will be analyzed through their design intention, Aristotle’s rhetorical appeals, point in time, and audience effectiveness, to determine whether or not the emblems were an appropriate and well-designed visual representation of the program to communicate to the world.

Aristotle's Rhetorical Appeals

In Ancient Greece, Aristotle formulated three concepts of, or appeals to, rhetoric: ethos, logos, and pathos. They were used to explain how rhetoric works and can now be utilized to analyze how well applied rhetoric is being employed.

Ethos can be referred to as the ethics of an argument, both in terms of the speaker’s credibility as well as their stance in the argument.

Logos is the logic of an argument: how it is formed and structured, and what facts are present.

Pathos can be thought of as the emotion in an argument. How emotion is presented by the speaker and felt by the audience.
